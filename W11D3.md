## 2. "The cloud" is someone else's computer (~15 min)

Strip the marketing and **the cloud is just computers you rent instead of own.** AWS runs enormous buildings full of servers (data centers); when you "deploy to S3," your files sit on disks in one of those buildings, and AWS handles the power, the cooling, the network, the broken-drive replacements. You pay for what you use and walk away when you're done.

This is **IaaS — Infrastructure as a Service.** You rent infrastructure (storage, compute, networking) as a service, on demand, instead of buying machines and racking them in a closet. The spectrum runs from "rent a bare server and do everything yourself" (most control, most work) to "hand over your files and let AWS serve them" (least control, least work). Your static site sits at the easy end: **you're not renting a running computer at all — just storage that hands out files.** That's the lightest possible footprint, and it's available to you *only because the site is static* (W9D3). A dynamic app would need a rented, running machine; you skipped that entire category.

The trade you made by going to the cloud: you gave up control (it's AWS's hardware, AWS's rules) and gained reach (it's online 24/7, from anywhere, without you running anything). For a public site that should be up whether or not your laptop is, that's the right trade.

## 3. Account, root, user, policy — the security model (~20 min)

Yesterday's "make a locked-down user instead of using root" wasn't fussiness; it's the core of how AWS keeps you safe. The model, in four nested pieces:

- **The account** is the whole rented space, tied to a billing relationship. Everything you make lives inside one account.
- **The root user** is the account's owner — the master key. It can do *anything*, including delete the account and spend without limit. You use it almost never (you used it once, to create your working user, and then put it away).
- **An IAM user** is an identity *inside* the account with only the permissions you grant it. `brand-lens-deploy` is a user. It exists so day-to-day work happens through a limited identity, not the master key.
- **A policy** is a list of permissions — statements of "this identity may perform these actions on these resources." `AmazonS3FullAccess` is a policy granting S3 actions. The public-read **bucket policy** you wrote is a policy too, attached to a *resource* (the bucket) instead of a user, saying "everyone may read these objects."

The principle tying it together is **least privilege**: every identity should hold the *minimum* permission its job requires. Why it matters concretely — if your laptop's key leaks, an attacker can do exactly what that key is allowed to do and *nothing else*. A root key leak loses the whole account; an S3-only user-key leak loses, at worst, your S3. You shrink the blast radius in advance, on purpose. (This is the same instinct as `.env` in W7 — a credential is a loaded thing; handle the minimum, expose the minimum.)

Two ideas, one sentence: **authentication** is *proving who you are* (your access key — `get-caller-identity` answered "you are `brand-lens-deploy`"); **authorization** is *what you're allowed to do once you've proven it* (your policies). The key gets you in the door; the policy says which rooms you can enter.

## 4. Regions — which building (~10 min)

When `aws configure` asked for a region and you typed `us-east-1`, you chose *which of AWS's data-center clusters* holds your stuff. A **region** is a geographic location — `us-east-1` is Northern Virginia, `us-west-2` is Oregon, `eu-west-1` is Ireland, and so on. Your bucket physically lives in the region you made it in.

Why it exists and when it matters:

- **Latency** — data served from a region near your visitors arrives faster. For a small brief site it's negligible; for a global app it's a real design choice.
- **It's part of the address.** You saw the region *inside* your website endpoint: `...s3-website-us-east-1.amazonaws.com`. The region isn't a setting off to the side — it's baked into where the thing actually is and how it's reached.
- **Consistency.** Tools default to your configured region; if you make a bucket in one region and your CLI is pointed at another, things "disappear" that are merely *elsewhere*. Pick one, stick to it.

The model: AWS isn't one place. It's many buildings around the world, and you're always working *in a specific one*.

## 5. Why your URL is http, not https (~10 min)

Yesterday's endpoint was `http://`, and you may have felt the itch — modern sites are `https://`, with the padlock. Here's the honest picture.

**HTTPS** is HTTP with encryption: it scrambles traffic between browser and server so nobody in between can read or tamper with it, and it proves the site is who it claims. It needs a **certificate**, and an S3 website endpoint doesn't provide one — it speaks plain `http://` only.

For Brand Lens *today*, plain HTTP is genuinely fine: the pages are public briefs with no logins, no forms, no secrets traveling back and forth. There's nothing private to protect in transit. HTTPS becomes non-negotiable the moment a site has a login, a payment, or any private data — none of which a read-only brief site has.

Getting HTTPS (and a custom domain like `brandlens.com` instead of the long S3 URL) means putting **CloudFront** — AWS's content-delivery network — in front of the bucket, with a certificate. That's a real, learnable next step, and it's deliberately *not* in this course: it's a Course-2 topic. Knowing *why* you'd reach for it, and that the gap is HTTPS + custom domain, is the understanding you need now.
